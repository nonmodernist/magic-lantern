VARIETY

Wednesday, August 16, 1939

Metro's production of "The Wizard of Oz" emerges as a magnificent screen fantasy, brilliantly conceived and executed with a technical wizardry that matches the magical elements of L. Frank Baum's beloved children's classic. This Technicolor spectacle, which has been in preparation for nearly two years, represents a triumphant achievement in motion picture artistry and establishes new standards for fantasy filmmaking.

Under the masterful direction of Victor Fleming, who took over the reins from George Cukor early in production, the film achieves a perfect balance between spectacle and storytelling. The decision to frame the Kansas sequences in sepia-toned photography before bursting into full Technicolor for the Land of Oz proves to be a stroke of genius, creating a visual metaphor that enhances the narrative's journey from mundane reality to extraordinary fantasy.

Young Judy Garland, in the role of Dorothy, delivers a performance of such genuine warmth and sincerity that she immediately captures the audience's heart. Her rendition of "Over the Rainbow," penned by Harold Arlen and E.Y. Harburg, is destined to become a standard, delivered with a wistful yearning that transcends the boundaries of children's entertainment to speak to audiences of all ages.

The supporting cast, led by Ray Bolger as the Scarecrow, Jack Haley as the Tin Man, and Bert Lahr as the Cowardly Lion, creates a trio of memorable characterizations that blend physical comedy with genuine pathos. Bolger's loose-limbed dancing, Haley's mechanical movements, and Lahr's over-the-top cowardice provide continuous entertainment while never losing sight of their characters' essential humanity.

Margaret Hamilton's portrayal of the Wicked Witch of the West sets a new standard for screen villainy, creating a character both terrifying and oddly compelling. Her green-hued makeup and cackling delivery will undoubtedly haunt the dreams of younger viewers while delighting their elders with its theatrical bravura.

From a technical standpoint, the film represents a watershed moment in Hollywood production. The tornado sequence, achieved through a combination of muslin wind socks and rear projection, creates a genuinely frightening natural disaster that serves as the perfect catalyst for Dorothy's journey. The Emerald City, realized through a combination of matte paintings and elaborate sets, sparkles with an otherworldly beauty that justifies every dollar of the reported $2.7 million budget.

Box office projections indicate that Metro has a substantial hit on its hands. Initial engagements in New York and Los Angeles have broken house records, with matinee showings particularly strong. The film's appeal to both children and adults suggests a long theatrical life, with particular strength expected during the holiday season...
